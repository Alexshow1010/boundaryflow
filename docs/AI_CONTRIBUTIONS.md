# AI Contributions

## Original Human Contribution

Alex originated BoundaryFlow and remained the source of the concept, product boundaries, safety principles, iteration decisions, and final approval.

Alex identified the central problem: a restraining order may remain a silent legal document until a violation is already escalating. Alex defined the dynamic, perceivable safety-boundary concept; the Protected Person App, Restricted Person Wearable, Authorized Console, and safety-engine model; the four boundary zones; the no-reverse-location-exposure rule; the ethical and legal limits; the MVP scope; and the required system interactions. Alex supplied and reviewed the original visual direction, evaluated behavior and presentation, and approved every major product and implementation decision.

## GPT-5.6 Contribution

GPT-5.6 was used as a product-reasoning, safety-architecture, visual-translation, and review layer.

It translated Alex’s concept into a formal system architecture, safety grammar, four-zone model, cross-device interaction model, and state flow. It defined responsibilities for the Protected App, Restricted Wearable, Authorized Console, Boundary Engine, Friction Engine, Safety Relay Engine, Violation Trace Engine, and Protective Zone Configuration Engine. It helped preserve the distinction between the future product and the Build Week simulator, converted visual boards into implementable specifications, produced detailed engineering and correction instructions, reviewed visual and interaction results, and refined public-submission language.

GPT-5.6 is not embedded as a live runtime model in the simulator. It does not monitor users, perform live risk analysis, judge behavior, or operate any deployed system.

## Codex Contribution

Codex was used inside VS Code as the software-engineering execution layer.

It structured the Next.js application; implemented React and TypeScript components; created the synchronized App, Boundary Field, Wearable, Console, timeline, and relay surfaces; implemented programmatic SVG/CSS boundary and sonar visuals; built distance-driven state transitions and Auto Demo controls; implemented Offline, Tamper, Resolve, Silent Notify, and One-Tap Help interactions; modeled local mock device and event data; fixed hydration and responsive issues; and performed lint, build, browser, interaction, and multi-viewport validation.

Codex also prepared repository documentation, release exclusions, verification scripts, and Vercel-ready project configuration.

## Iterative Development Workflow

```text
Alex
  ↓
Original concept, product boundaries, safety principles, and final decisions

GPT-5.6
  ↓
Product architecture, safety grammar, visual translation, interaction specifications, and review

Codex
  ↓
Software implementation, debugging, testing, validation, and deployment preparation

BoundaryFlow Cross-Device System Simulator
```

This project was not produced through a single prompt. It emerged through an iterative loop:

1. Alex defined the product intent, constraints, and acceptance criteria.
2. GPT-5.6 translated that direction into architecture, safety rules, visual language, and implementation specifications.
3. Codex implemented the current increment and ran technical verification.
4. Alex and GPT-5.6 reviewed behavior, screenshots, recordings, safety implications, and product quality.
5. Codex corrected the implementation and repeated validation.
6. Alex made the final scope and approval decisions.

## Visual Reference Assets

Alex supplied the original visual direction and reviewed the preconstruction boards in `01_preconstruction_boards/`. Curated generated reference sheets are stored in `02_generated_asset_packs/`.

The complete boards and asset sheets are not embedded as finished application screens. Codex translated their visual cues into reusable React, CSS, and programmatic SVG surfaces. GPT-5.6 helped interpret the boards and specify hierarchy, synchronized states, boundary visualization, and responsive behavior.

## Manual Review and Final Approval

Alex manually reviewed the evolving simulator, identified priority corrections, controlled scope, and retained final authority over product direction and release approval. GPT-5.6 supported product, visual, interaction, and safety evaluation. Automated linting, builds, and browser checks supplemented but did not replace human review.

## Simulated Features

- Relative distance and boundary levels
- App and wearable states
- Vibration, audio, indicator, battery, pairing, and compliance displays
- Offline and tamper conditions
- Case, timeline, relay, escalation, and evidence records
- Silent Notify and One-Tap Help actions
- Auto Demo transitions
- Retreat and resolution

No simulated action sends a real notification, locates a real person, contacts an authority, or controls physical hardware.

## Safety Boundaries

- No protected-person precise location or reverse location exposure
- No public or vigilante tracking
- No private self-created monitoring targets
- No automated guilt determination or predictive policing
- No real court, law-enforcement, emergency-service, or evidence-chain claim
- Judicial or authorized activation only in the intended future product model
- Human legal, privacy, security, accessibility, and safety review required before real-world use

See [SAFETY_BOUNDARIES.md](SAFETY_BOUNDARIES.md).

## Current Limitations

- Local mock data only
- No manufactured wearable
- No live GPS or cellular connection
- No backend, database, production authentication, or identity verification
- No real alert delivery or evidence chain
- No external-authority integration
- No legal or operational safety certification
- No live GPT-5.6 runtime

## Summary

GPT-5.6 was used as a product-reasoning, safety-architecture, visual-translation, and review layer. Codex was used as the software-engineering execution layer. Alex remained the source of the concept, product boundaries, safety principles, iteration decisions, and final approval.
